Flight Data Acquisition for Amateur Solar and Lunar Transit Photography:
A Cost–Benefit Analysis of Available Data Sources
Operational Economics of Real-Time Aircraft Tracking Under Budget Constraints
Flymoon v1.0 FlightAware AeroAPI Personal Tier OpenSky Network ADS-B / RTL-SDR
Abstract. Amateur astronomers seeking to photograph aircraft transiting the solar or lunar disk require accurate, real-time aircraft position data to predict and prepare for sub-second transit events. This paper examines four data acquisition strategies — FlightAware AeroAPI exclusively, OpenSky Network exclusively, a hybrid model combining free positional data with selective paid enrichment, and a locally-deployed ADS-B receiver — with respect to operational cost, data fidelity, latency, and suitability for automated transit detection. We demonstrate that the exclusive use of FlightAware AeroAPI at typical polling intervals produces costs ranging from $14–$87/month, substantially exceeding the $5/month Personal tier credit, while the hybrid approach typically remains within the included $5/month free credit — effectively free — without material degradation of transit detection capability.

1. Introduction

The detection and capture of aircraft transiting the disk of the Sun or Moon requires solving a real-time angular separation problem: given a continuous stream of aircraft positions and a known celestial body position, identify those trajectories whose projected path will bring an aircraft within the angular diameter of the target (~0.5°) within the near-term prediction window (~15 minutes).

This computation is inexpensive. The data acquisition, however, is not. Aircraft positions must be refreshed frequently enough that the prediction model — which assumes constant velocity and heading — remains accurate over the 15-minute window. Insufficient refresh rates introduce heading and speed errors that cause transits to be missed or generate false positives requiring operator attention.

The FlightAware AeroAPI, which provides the richest available flight metadata, prices its service on a per-result-set basis. As we demonstrate below, this pricing model interacts poorly with the high-frequency polling required for transit detection. The central contribution of this analysis is a cost model that quantifies this tension and proposes mitigation strategies available to budget-conscious observers.

2. The FlightAware AeroAPI Pricing Model

FlightAware charges per result set, where one result set contains up to 15 flight records. The current Personal tier rate is $0.02 per result set, with a monthly credit of $5.00 — sufficient for exactly 250 result sets before additional charges apply.

Flymoon's bounding-box search uses the /flights/search endpoint with max_pages=1, returning a single result set per call regardless of how many aircraft are present (up to 15). Therefore:

Monthly budget at $5.00 credit = 250 calls maximum.

2.2 Effect of Active-Hours Restriction

Transit detection is only meaningful when the Sun or Moon is above the observer's horizon and above local obstruction angles. In mid-latitudes, this typically limits the usable window to 6–10 hours per day. With the quadrant min-angle masking feature, polling can be suppressed when the target is below the obstruction threshold, reducing effective daily call counts.

5. The Hybrid Strategy in Detail

The hybrid approach exploits a fundamental asymmetry in information requirements: continuous position data is needed for all aircraft at all times to compute angular separation, but rich flight metadata (aircraft type, airline, origin, destination) is only operationally significant when a transit is actually imminent.

The proposed implementation proceeds as follows:

  1. Every ~60 seconds — Query OpenSky Network bounding-box endpoint (free). Extract: ICAO24 hex, callsign, latitude, longitude, altitude, heading, speed, vertical rate.
  2. Continuously — Compute angular separation between each aircraft's predicted 15-minute trajectory and the current Sun/Moon position.
  3. On first HIGH-probability detection (alt_diff ≤ 1° AND az_diff ≤ 1°) for a given fa_flight_id — Issue a single FlightAware /flights/{ident} call to retrieve aircraft type, airline, and route.
  4. Cache the enrichment for the duration of the flight (~2–3 hours, keyed by ICAO24 hex or callsign). Subsequent alerts for the same aircraft incur no additional cost.
Key insight: Transit events are rare. A busy 3°×3° bounding box over San Diego may contain 50–150 aircraft at any given moment, but only 1–5 per day will achieve HIGH-probability geometry with the Sun or Moon. The hybrid approach pays FlightAware only for those rare events.

6. The ADS-B Receiver Strategy

A software-defined radio (SDR) receiver decodes ADS-B transponder broadcasts directly from passing aircraft. At cruise altitude, ADS-B signals are receivable at ranges exceeding 250 nautical miles. For an observer in San Diego, this encompasses the entirety of the Southern California TCA, all trans-Pacific approach corridors, and the major north-south airway system — more than sufficient coverage for transit detection.

Commercial-grade RTL-SDR dongles are available for $25–$35. Software decoders (dump1090, readsb) run on a Raspberry Pi 4 (~$35–$55) or directly on the user's computer. Combined with the same selective FA enrichment strategy, monthly operational costs approach zero after the one-time hardware investment.

⭐ Recommended for committed observers: ADS-B + selective FA enrichment provides the best possible transit detection capability — sub-second latency, unlimited refresh rate, and near-zero ongoing cost — at an accessible one-time hardware cost of approximately $60–$90.

8. Conclusion

The FlightAware AeroAPI Personal tier's result-set pricing model is poorly matched to the high-frequency polling requirements of automated transit detection. Running Flymoon 24/7 at a 10-minute interval costs approximately $86/month — 17 times the included $5 credit. Even the most aggressive cost reduction measures available within the FA-only model (60-minute intervals, active-hours masking) leave observers with either unacceptable positional staleness or marginal budget compliance.

The hybrid strategy — using the free OpenSky Network API for continuous position data and reserving FlightAware calls for confirmed HIGH-probability transit events — typically generates fewer than 250 FA calls per month, keeping costs within the included $5/month Personal tier credit and making Hybrid effectively free. Full flight metadata is preserved for the alerts that matter most. The selective FA enrichment cache ensures that each unique aircraft is queried at most once per flight, regardless of how many transit alerts it generates.

For observers willing to invest in hardware, a local ADS-B receiver eliminates all ongoing position data costs entirely, provides sub-second update latency, and functions without internet connectivity — representing the optimal long-term solution for dedicated transit photographers.
